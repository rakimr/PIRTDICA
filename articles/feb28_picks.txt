SCOTTIE BARNES: Points + Rebounds vs Washington

TOR @ WAS | Power Forward | Point Forward

Barnes averages 19.1 points and 8.2 rebounds per game on 34.3 minutes and 24.2% usage. His per 100 possessions rates of 27.6 points and 11.7 rebounds per 100 tell you he produces at a high volume relative to every minute on the floor. He is classified as a Point Forward archetype, a player who initiates offense from the forward position while crashing the glass. Tonight he faces Washington, the worst defense in the league against nearly every archetype classification.

His shot chart reveals a player who attacks the paint relentlessly. 35.9% of his attempts come at the restricted area and 28.5% in the paint, giving him a combined 64.4% rim-and-paint shot distribution. His shot creation data confirms it: 55.7% paint production, 21.1% pull-up shooting, and 22.2% catch-and-shoot. Only 20.2% of his total attempts come from three, and 80.6% of those threes are catch-and-shoot. Barnes is not a perimeter scorer. He is a paint-first player who gets to the rim through drives and cuts, and who collects offensive rebounds by virtue of already being in the paint when shots go up.

Washington's defense is the matchup that unlocks this. The DVA data shows Washington allows a +0.065 FPPM differential to Point Forwards, with the scoring component at +0.070 and the rebounding component at +0.047. Those are the two largest positive components in Washington's Point Forward matchup profile. Their DVP score of 52.7 against opposing power forwards is the worst in the league for that position. Washington gives up positive FPPM differentials to every single archetype, the only team on tonight's slate where that is true across the board.

Washington is missing Alex Sarr, Trae Young, and D'Angelo Russell. Their depleted frontcourt means Tristan Vukcevic, Bilal Coulibaly, and the remaining bigs absorb the interior defensive load. Sarr's absence is critical for rebounding: his 7-foot-1 frame and activity on the glass are Washington's best tools for limiting opponent boards, and without him, Barnes faces a softer frontcourt in the paint. Toronto is a 13.5-point road favorite, which creates blowout risk for fourth-quarter minutes, but Barnes's 34.3 minutes per game average suggests he plays the majority of the first three quarters regardless of score.

Barnes's defensive profile adds secondary value. His 4.45 deflections per 48 minutes and 8.56 contested shots per 48 tell you he is active on the defensive end, which means he is engaged and positioned for long defensive rebounds that transition into fastbreak opportunities. His 2.1 blocks per game with a 2.1 per 100 rate further confirms the defensive activity level.

The stat to watch: Points over 17.5 is the primary play. Barnes's 27.6 per 100 rate projects comfortably above this line in standard minutes, and Washington's +0.070 scoring differential to Point Forwards adds further margin. The book has this at -120/-106, which tells you the market sees it as close. The DVA data says it is not close. The secondary angle is rebounds over 8.5 at -102/-125. His 11.7 per 100 rate against a Washington frontcourt missing Alex Sarr creates a clear path above the line. He is classified as a Point Forward archetype, the same classification as Barnes, but his playmaking operates on a different axis. Where Barnes creates through paint penetration and kick-outs, LeBron orchestrates the entire half-court offense as the primary initiator.

His shot chart shows a versatile scorer: 35.9% at the restricted area, 18.5% in the paint, 17.2% from midrange, and 28.4% from three. That balance is what makes his passing so dangerous. Defenses cannot commit to taking away any single zone, which means every drive creates a decision point for the help defender. When the help comes, LeBron finds the open man. When it does not, he finishes at the rim. His shot creation data shows 17.6% pull-up shooting frequency, confirming he operates as a primary ball handler who creates off the dribble rather than catching and shooting.

Golden State's DVA data shows they allow a +0.074 FPPM differential to Point Forwards, with the assist component at +0.047 the largest single piece. Golden State's DVP score of 47.7 against opposing point guards is middling, but against opposing small forwards it drops to 42.9, one of the better marks in the league. The DVA archetype data tells a different story than the positional DVP: Golden State struggles specifically against Point Forwards who facilitate, not just forwards who score.

LeBron's teammates are built to convert his passes. Luka Doncic (37.7% usage) draws constant defensive attention, which creates the spacing LeBron needs.
