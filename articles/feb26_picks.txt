Atlanta's teammate context reinforces the setup. Dyson Daniels (6.1 apg), Nickeil Alexander-Walker (3.8 apg), and CJ McCollum (3.5 apg) all handle the ball, but Johnson is the primary creator. DYSON DANIELS: Steals vs Washington

ATL @ WAS | Shooting Guard | Scoring Wing

Every deflected pass or dribble is a potential turnover, and Daniels creates those disruptions at an elite rate. His 84.2% rim-and-paint shot distribution on offense confirms he is a slasher who lives inside, which means on the defensive end he is positioned in the passing lanes between the perimeter and the paint, the exact zone where steals happen.

Washington is the perfect opponent for this. The Wizards have the worst record in the league at 8-41 and turn the ball over at one of the highest rates in the NBA. Their roster tonight is severely depleted: no Trae Young, no D'Angelo Russell, no De'Andre Hunter, no Alex Sarr. The DVA data shows Washington allows a +0.005 steal differential to Scoring Wings specifically. That number looks small, but the broader context amplifies it. Atlanta is a 10.5-point road favorite. Large leads create garbage-time possessions where backups press and play aggressively, which generates additional turnover opportunities. The stat to watch: Steals 2+ is the primary play. Daniels averages 1.9 per game, which means he hits 2+ steals in roughly half his games under normal circumstances. Tonight's circumstances are not normal. Washington's depleted backcourt with backup ball handlers against one of the league's most disruptive perimeter defenders in a 10.5-point blowout environment is the structural setup for a multi-steal game. The 1+ alternate line is the safer play but likely priced accordingly. The edge here is in the 2+ threshold, where the matchup and game environment push a coin-flip outcome toward a favorable probability.


VICTOR WEMBANYAMA: Blocks vs Brooklyn

His hustle data confirms the activity level: 13.3 contested shots per 48 minutes and 4.1 deflections per 48. He is constantly in position to challenge shots at the rim, and his length means he does not need to be perfectly positioned. His contest radius extends further than any other player in the league.

Brooklyn's offensive profile feeds directly into Wembanyama's blocking ability. Their defensive shot zone data shows opponents attempt 32.5% of their shots at the restricted area, the highest frequency on tonight's slate for teams facing San Antonio. Brooklyn's offense functions similarly: they are a team that attacks the paint through drives and cuts rather than settling for perimeter shots. Nic Claxton, Michael Porter Jr., and Noah Clowney all profile as interior players who finish at the rim. Those finishes are exactly the shots Wembanyama blocks.

The DVA data for Brooklyn against Stretch 5 archetypes shows a +0.035 FPPM differential with a +0.009 block differential specifically. That number is modest, but Wembanyama's baseline production is so high that even a marginal matchup advantage produces significant block volume. His 4.5 per 100 rate in 29 minutes of action projects to approximately 2.6 blocks before any matchup adjustment.

San Antonio is a 12.5-point road favorite. The blowout risk is real for total production: if the game is decided early, Wembanyama could sit the fourth quarter. A 24-minute night still projects to approximately 2.2 blocks at his per 100 rate.

His shot chart shows 33.3% of his own attempts at the restricted area and 30.7% from three. The stat to watch: Blocks over 2.5 is the primary play. Wembanyama's 4.5 per 100 rate is elite enough that even with reduced minutes in a blowout, the projection clears the line. Brooklyn's paint-heavy offense creates the volume of rim attempts he needs to accumulate blocks. The 2+ line is the safer floor, but the over 2.5 is where the real value sits because his season average of 2.9 already clears it and the books often set it closer to the average rather than the matchup-adjusted projection. In his 40 games this season, he has hit 3+ blocks in over half of them. The Brooklyn interior attack just needs to give him the opportunities, and their offensive structure guarantees it.
